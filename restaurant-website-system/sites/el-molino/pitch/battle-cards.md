# El Molino Battle Cards

Use these as quick responses during the pitch. Keep the tone calm and practical.

## Card 1: "We Already Have A Website"

**Likely concern:** They see the current site as "good enough" because it has the logo, announcement, menu PDF, address, and phone.

**Response:**  
"You do have a site. The issue is that it is doing announcement work, not guest-conversion work. The new site makes the menu readable, shows hours and visit actions, explains the new management chapter warmly, and brings the guest proof into the page."

**Proof points:**

- Current site is mostly a one-page new-ownership announcement.
- Menu is a PDF.
- Hours were not visible in the captured current-site DOM.
- Reviews and renovated-room proof were not used.

**Close:**  
"This is not replacing the existence of a site. It is upgrading the job the site does."

## Card 2: "We Just Need People To See The Menu"

**Likely concern:** They want the website to stay simple and menu-first.

**Response:**  
"Agreed. That is exactly why the PDF cannot be the only menu path. A phone user should be able to scan sections without pinching and panning."

**Proof points:**

- Prototype keeps the PDF as fallback.
- HTML highlights make the menu easier to read.
- Final build can make prices and add-ons clearer.

**Close:**  
"The PDF can stay. It just should not be the front door."

## Card 3: "We Do Not Want To Overpromise With AI"

**Likely concern:** AI could invent answers or create service risk.

**Response:**  
"The concierge is intentionally narrow. It answers from restaurant facts: hours, address, menu highlights, new-management story, and visit information. It is not an ordering, payment, or reservation system."

**Proof points:**

- Server route keeps the key out of the browser.
- Prompt tells it not to invent prices, policies, or guarantees.
- Time-sensitive questions point guests to call.
- Footer tells guests to call for exact timing, reservations, or groups.

**Close:**  
"Think of it as a host-style guide, not automation replacing the restaurant."

## Card 4: "What If The AI Is Wrong?"

**Likely concern:** Accuracy and reputation.

**Response:**  
"We keep the AI grounded in approved site content and remove anything the owner has not signed off on. For launch, the safer version only answers from hours, menu highlights, visit info, and approved story."

**Proof points:**

- No booking commitments.
- No allergy guarantees.
- No live pricing claims without owner approval.
- Can be disabled or hidden instantly if needed.

**Close:**  
"The risk is controllable because the AI is scoped. The site still works without it."

## Card 5: "The Current Ownership Wording Is Important"

**Likely concern:** They need guests to know this is new management with no direct relation to prior ownership.

**Response:**  
"We kept that message, but made it warmer and more confidence-building. The page says new ownership and management under the Zepeda family, while also reminding guests this is still the Carpentersville landmark they know."

**Proof points:**

- Banner appears directly under the header.
- Hero says new Zepeda-family ownership and management.
- Story section explains the new family chapter.

**Close:**  
"The site can be clear about new management without making the first impression feel defensive."

## Card 6: "We Are Worried About Negative Reviews"

**Likely concern:** Pricing/upcharge complaints and old-customer comparison.

**Response:**  
"The site should help reduce that friction. The best response is not hiding reviews. It is giving guests clearer menu structure, clearer add-on language, and a warmer story for what changed."

**Proof points:**

- Menu section is built to become a clearer HTML menu.
- Reviews carousel focuses on patterns: room, food, service, tortillas, bar.
- Negative pricing themes become a launch checklist item for menu clarity.

**Close:**  
"The rebuild can turn a review risk into an operations fix."

## Card 7: "We Need More Photos First"

**Likely concern:** They may not have food/owner images ready.

**Response:**  
"The prototype works with current interior photos because the renovated room is part of the story. For launch, a short photo pass would make it much stronger."

**Proof points:**

- Current site has usable room/interior shots.
- Missing: food close-ups, salsa/tortilla process, margaritas, exterior, owner/team portrait.
- Photo ask is only 15-20 shots.

**Close:**  
"We can pitch direction now and upgrade the photo layer before launch."

## Card 8: "Why Not Just Use Social Media?"

**Likely concern:** Facebook/Instagram/Maps already provide updates.

**Response:**  
"Social is where people discover you. The website is where they decide. It needs to answer menu, hours, story, directions, phone, and confidence questions without sending people bouncing across platforms."

**Proof points:**

- Owned site controls the story.
- Site can be linked from Maps, social, QR codes, and ads.
- Guests can still use social; the website becomes the canonical home.

**Close:**  
"Social feeds attention. The website converts it."

## Card 9: "We Do Not Want It To Feel Too Fancy"

**Likely concern:** Over-luxury design could alienate regulars.

**Response:**  
"This design is intentionally heritage casual, not upscale destination. It uses the renovated room, warmer colors, clear menu sections, and practical actions."

**Proof points:**

- Copy leads with tortillas, salsas, margaritas, family chapter.
- Menu and call actions stay prominent.
- Review proof remains approachable.

**Close:**  
"It should feel like the room got warmer, not like the restaurant became something else."

## Card 10: "Can This Help With SEO?"

**Likely concern:** More search visibility.

**Response:**  
"Yes, especially once the menu becomes real HTML. Search engines can read page text, item names, hours, address, and story much better than a tiny PDF-only menu."

**Proof points:**

- HTML menu sections.
- Crawlable restaurant story.
- Address/phone/hours in page content.
- Better canonical domain setup.

**Close:**  
"The biggest SEO unlock is replacing PDF-only menu behavior with structured page content."

## Card 11: "What About The Old Domain?"

**Likely concern:** Existing customer links and Maps references.

**Response:**  
"Part of the launch checklist is choosing the canonical domain and redirecting or updating old paths. The goal is one trusted destination."

**Proof points:**

- Audit found current site on `thenewmolino.com`.
- Legacy indexed domain had DNS issues during the audit.
- Google/Maps/menu paths should be cleaned up before launch.

**Close:**  
"The site should not make guests guess which El Molino link is real."

## Card 12: "How Do We Explain Price?"

**Likely concern:** Menu upcharges and perception.

**Response:**  
"The new site gives us a place to make pricing clearer before a guest arrives. Final menu work should include add-ons, premium proteins, and any service-charge language."

**Proof points:**

- Some recent complaints centered on surprise charges.
- Clear menu structure can prevent confusion.
- Staff should not have to solve every pricing question at the table.

**Close:**  
"Clear pricing is part of hospitality."

## Card 13: "Can We Launch Fast?"

**Likely concern:** Timing.

**Response:**  
"The prototype is pitch-ready now. Launch speed depends on owner signoff, final menu/prices, photo approval, domain decision, and reservation/order links."

**Fast-launch path:**

- Keep current interior photos.
- Launch menu highlights + PDF fallback.
- Use phone/directions as primary CTAs.
- Keep concierge scoped to basic facts.

**Better-launch path:**

- Add 15-20 new photos.
- Build full HTML menu with prices.
- Connect real reservation/order links.
- Finalize domain redirects.

**Close:**  
"We can launch lean or launch stronger. The content decisions drive the timeline."

## Card 14: "Why Pay For This Instead Of A Template?"

**Likely concern:** Cost.

**Response:**  
"The value is not just layout. It is the restaurant-specific story, review proof, menu clarity, mobile path, live status, and AI host all shaped around El Molino's actual new chapter."

**Proof points:**

- Uses restaurant audit data.
- Reflects Zepeda-family management and 1984 landmark status.
- Solves PDF menu friction.
- Adds live open status and concierge.

**Close:**  
"A template gives pages. This gives El Molino's new chapter a sales surface."

## Card 15: "What Do We Need From You?"

**Use when the meeting turns into next steps.**

Ask for:

- Final menu and prices.
- Add-on/upcharge rules.
- Final hours.
- Canonical domain decision.
- Reservation/order link decision.
- Owner-approved new-management wording.
- Photo pass or permission to launch with current interior photos.
- Approval of AI concierge scope.

Close:

"Once those are approved, this becomes a launch project instead of a prototype."
