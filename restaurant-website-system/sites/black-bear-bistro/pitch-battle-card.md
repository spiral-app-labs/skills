# Black Bear Bistro — Sales Battle Card

## Sales Position

Do not lead with "your Squarespace site is weak." Lead with:

> You already have the ingredients guests care about: a chef-owned story, an adventurous menu, vegan options, pickup ordering, and a small dining room worth booking. The new site puts those decisions in the right order.

Black Bear does not need a fake luxury treatment. It needs a cleaner bistro site that makes the menu and chef story easy to understand before the guest hits a PDF or starts hunting for contact details.

## Fast Diagnosis

| Current Site Problem | Business Impact | Redesign Answer |
|---|---|---|
| Hero leans on a customer quote instead of restaurant-authored positioning | The restaurant lets someone else define the brand | New homepage leads with chef-driven New American cooking in downtown Algonquin |
| Chef Santiago's credibility is underused | The strongest trust signal is not doing sales work | About page and hero copy make Santiago and Estela part of the reason to book |
| Menu lives behind PDFs | Diners cannot quickly scan dishes, prices, vegan options, or specials | Homepage turns the menu into the main product with inline sections |
| TableAgent booking exists but is under-surfaced | After-hours mobile guests may not commit | Header and hero make "Book table" a primary action |
| Online ordering exists but feels separate | Pickup revenue is one click too far away | Redesign keeps Square ordering visible without trying to replace it |

## Strongest Purchase Reasons

1. **The menu is the hook.**  
   Black Bear has wild game, seafood, vegan/vegetarian options, specials, and bistro staples. Hiding that behind PDFs makes the guest work too hard.

2. **The chef story can create trust before the guest reads reviews.**  
   Santiago and Estela are owner-operator signals. The site should make the restaurant feel personal, not template-generic.

3. **Booking and ordering should be obvious, not separate errands.**  
   The redesign keeps TableAgent booking, menu browsing, pickup ordering, hours, and location in one flow, while using the 4.83/5 TableAgent rating from 1,537 votes as a trust signal.

4. **The site should match how people choose dinner now.**  
   Most guests compare restaurants on phones. They want to see food, price range, availability, and vibe quickly. The new homepage answers that in order.

5. **The implementation avoids unnecessary operational disruption.**  
   Square ordering stays. Existing phone/email stays. Reservation links can stay external. The redesign improves the decision path without forcing a system migration.

## Best Opening Line

> The opportunity is not just a better-looking site. It is making the menu, chef story, TableAgent proof, and table-booking path do the selling before a guest leaves the page.

## Walkthrough Script

1. **Open the homepage.**  
   Show the shift from review-quote positioning to a clear restaurant-owned statement: chef-driven New American dinner in downtown Algonquin.

2. **Point to the CTAs.**  
   "Book table," "Our menu," and "Order pickup" map to the three actual diner intents.

3. **Scroll the inline menu.**  
   Emphasize that dishes and prices are visible on the page. This is the core business case.

4. **Open the about page.**  
   Position Santiago and Estela as the trust engine. Say: "People do not just buy dinner; they buy confidence that the room and kitchen know what they are doing."

5. **Open contact.**  
   Show that catering, events, allergies, map, phone, and hours are practical and easy to find.

## Objection Counters

### "We already have Squarespace."

**Counter:**  
Squarespace is not the issue by itself. The issue is the order of information. The redesign puts the highest-converting information first: menu, chef story, booking, ordering, hours, and location.

### "People can just download the menu PDFs."

**Counter:**  
Some will, but many will not. PDFs are slower on mobile and bad for quick comparison. The new site lets guests scan the menu instantly, then still download PDFs if they want the full source.

### "We do not want to change online ordering."

**Counter:**  
Good. We should keep Square ordering. The redesign is not an ordering-system migration; it is a better front door that sends guests to Square when pickup is the intent.

### "We are a small restaurant; we do not need a big site."

**Counter:**  
Exactly. This is not a big site. It is a focused bistro site: homepage/menu, about, contact, booking, order link, and location. Small room, clear decisions.

### "Our customers know the food is good."

**Counter:**  
Returning customers do. New guests do not. The site's job is to give new diners enough confidence to book without needing to read five review platforms first.

### "We do not have time for a huge project."

**Counter:**  
The project can stay tight because the business pieces already exist: menu PDFs, Square ordering, photos, hours, phone, email, and location. We are reorganizing and sharpening what is already there.

## Sensitive Claims

Verify before saying in client-facing copy:

- Exact number of years Santiago spent at 1776 Crystal Lake
- Exact role/title at 1776
- Current preference for TableAgent versus any alternate booking flow
- Which specials should stay evergreen versus dated

In the sales conversation, you can say:

> Your chef background is a major trust signal. We should make sure the exact wording is right, then put it where guests will actually see it.

## Do Not Say

- "Your site looks cheap."
- "PDF menus are embarrassing."
- "You need to become upscale."
- "We should replace Square."
- "You have no online reservations."
- "We know your chef history for sure" before verifying details.

## Say Instead

- "The current site makes guests work too hard to see the menu."
- "The chef story should be a front-page trust signal."
- "Your TableAgent proof should be visible before guests bounce."
- "We can keep the systems you already use."
- "This is a clearer front door, not a disruptive rebuild."

## Close

> Black Bear already has the hard part: a real menu, a chef-owned story, pickup ordering, and a small-room dinner experience. The redesign makes those strengths visible in the order guests need them so more visitors turn into bookings, pickup orders, and confident first visits.

## Proposed Ask

Sell a focused bistro redesign package:

- Homepage with inline menu, booking CTA, pickup CTA, hours, and specials area
- About page built around Santiago and Estela's story
- Contact page for catering, events, large parties, allergies, map, and phone/email
- Square ordering preserved as the pickup flow
- TableAgent reservation link surfaced prominently, unless the owner confirms a different provider
