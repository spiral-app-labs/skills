# nina-binky-in-app-resource-writing

Use this when Nina is writing a Binky resource article meant to help a tired new parent inside the app, not chase search traffic.

## Required Input Brief
Start from these fields:
- **topic**
- **audience**: pregnant, postpartum, newborn, partner, both parents, etc.
- **goal**: reassure, explain, coach, normalize, de-escalate, or prompt a conversation
- **product context**: where this article sits in Binky and what screen/flow it supports
- **CTA or next step**: another article, a check-in, a conversation prompt, a save/share action
- **constraints**: word target, tone notes, medical caution, claims to avoid

If part of the brief is missing, make the safest assumption and state it at the top.

## Binky Standard
Every in-app article should feel:
- warm but not syrupy
- clear on a first tired read
- emotionally intelligent
- specific and practical
- respectful of both partners
- calm about hard moments

Avoid:
- bloggy filler
- SEO sludge
- long paragraphs
- shamey advice
- hard product selling
- medical certainty where we do not have it

## Core Job
The article should do at least one of these well:
- help a parent feel less alone
- explain what is happening in plain language
- give one or two practical next moves
- reduce friction between partners
- point the reader toward a better next conversation

## Writing Workflow
1. **Define the emotional moment**
   - what is the parent likely feeling right now?
   - what would make them feel seen fast?

2. **Define the practical outcome**
   - what should the reader understand, do, or say after reading?

3. **Keep the scope tight**
   - one article = one problem
   - do not sprawl into a generic parenting essay

4. **Write for mobile**
   - short paragraphs
   - useful subheads
   - bullets where they genuinely help
   - front-load the most reassuring/helpful line

5. **Connect back to Binky lightly**
   - mention the value of reflection, check-ins, repair prompts, or shared understanding only when it fits naturally
   - the product should feel like support, not the point of the article

## Default Output Structure
### 1) draft header
- topic
- audience
- article goal
- product context
- CTA / next step
- confidence or source note

### 2) article package
- **title**
- **one-line summary**
- **suggested placement/context note**

### 3) outline
Show the section structure before the full draft.

### 4) full draft
Default pattern:
- opening line that makes the parent feel understood
- quick explanation of the issue or moment
- practical guidance or reframing
- partner/relationship angle when relevant
- next step

### 5) CTA block
Give the exact in-app CTA copy or next-step copy.

### 6) source / confidence block
List sources used or note that the piece is experience-based / low-claim editorial guidance.

## Style Rules
- lead with the human moment, not an abstract thesis
- prefer simple language over expert-sounding language
- write like a smart, caring coach sitting next to the reader
- include specific examples when they make the advice easier to act on
- if the topic is emotionally charged, steady the tone instead of dramatizing it

## Safety + Trust Rules
- do not invent statistics
- do not overstate medical or developmental claims
- include “talk to your doctor / clinician” guidance when the topic reasonably calls for it
- distinguish between common experiences and urgent warning signs
- if evidence is weak, say so plainly

## Good Binky Angles
Useful article angles often include:
- what this feeling means
- what is normal vs what deserves attention
- how to talk about it with your partner
- what to try tonight
- how to lower pressure instead of perform perfection

## CTA Rules
Good CTAs for in-app articles:
- gentle and specific
- connected to the article's emotional/job-to-be-done
- low-friction

Examples:
- try a check-in on this tonight
- save this for the next rough evening
- send this to your partner and talk through one part together
- read the companion guide on [related topic]

## Final Quality Check
Before handing off, verify:
- the article clearly serves one reader moment
- the tone feels like Binky, not generic wellness content
- the draft is easy to read on a phone
- the advice is practical, not vague
- any sensitive claims are careful
- the CTA fits naturally
- the piece can ship without extra prompting

## Handoff Format
Return one clean package with:
1. assumptions
2. title + summary
3. outline
4. full draft
5. CTA copy
6. source/confidence notes

If you are choosing between clever and clear, pick clear every time.
